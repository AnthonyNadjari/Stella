<Section number="01" title="Problem Statement">

A basket option written on $n$ underlyings with weights $w_i$ has a payoff driven by the **aggregate move** of the basket. When pricing exotic derivatives on baskets — dispersion trades, worst-of structures, rainbow options — a key input is the **implied volatility surface of the basket itself**.

In practice, liquid vanilla options exist on the individual underlyings, not on the basket directly. The task is to **reconstruct the basket's implied vol surface** — and in particular its **skew** — from single-stock information and a correlation structure.

<Callout type="insight">
The basket skew is not a simple weighted average of single-stock skews. It depends non-linearly on correlations, and the reconstruction error can be significant for short-dated options or highly skewed underlyings.
</Callout>

The basket price at time $T$ is:

$$
B_T = \sum_{i=1}^n w_i S_i(T)
$$

with $\sum_i w_i = 1$. We define the basket log-return as $X_B = \log(B_T / B_0)$.

</Section>

<Section number="02" title="First-Order Approximation">

Under a **Gaussian correlation model** (all underlyings lognormal), the basket variance is:

$$
\sigma_B^2 \approx \sum_{i,j} w_i w_j \rho_{ij} \sigma_i \sigma_j
$$

<MathBlock label="Basket ATM Variance" number="2.1">

$$
\sigma_B^2 = \mathbf{w}^\top \Sigma \mathbf{w}
$$

where $\Sigma_{ij} = \rho_{ij}\,\sigma_i\,\sigma_j$ is the covariance matrix.

</MathBlock>

The **first-order skew** of the basket derives from a cumulant expansion. Define the skewness of stock $i$ as $\kappa_3^{(i)}$. Then:

$$
\kappa_3^B \approx \frac{\sum_{i,j,k} w_i w_j w_k \,C_{ijk}}{\sigma_B^3}
$$

where $C_{ijk} = \mathbb{E}[\delta X_i \, \delta X_j \, \delta X_k]$ is the **third-order co-cumulant** tensor.

<Callout type="warning">
The co-cumulant tensor $C_{ijk}$ is rarely observable directly. In practice, one assumes a **diagonal approximation** where cross-stock third moments are set to zero. This underestimates basket skew when correlations are high.
</Callout>

</Section>

<Section number="03" title="Skew via Moment Matching">

A cleaner method is to **match moments** of a parametric distribution to those computed from the basket's cumulants.

**Step 1** — Compute basket moments up to order 4 via Isserlis' theorem.

**Step 2** — Fit a **Gram-Charlier expansion** of the basket density:

<MathBlock label="Gram-Charlier Density" number="3.1">

$$
f_B(x) = \phi(x)\left[1 + \frac{\kappa_3^B}{6}H_3(x) + \frac{\kappa_4^B}{24}H_4(x) + \cdots \right]
$$

where $H_k$ are Hermite polynomials and $\phi$ is the standard normal density.

</MathBlock>

**Step 3** — Extract the implied vol smile. The basket skew approximation:

$$
\sigma_{\text{imp}}(k) \approx \sigma_B\left[1 + \frac{\kappa_3^B}{6}k + \frac{\kappa_4^B - 3}{24}k^2\right]
$$

where $k = \log(K/F)/\sigma_B\sqrt{T}$ is the normalized moneyness.

<Callout type="result" title="KEY RESULT">
The basket skew is proportional to $\kappa_3^B$. For a basket of negatively skewed stocks under positive correlation, the basket skew is weaker than the average single-stock skew — a fundamental result for dispersion trading.
</Callout>

</Section>

<Section number="04" title="Correlation Weighting of Skew">

One of the most important practical results: **higher correlation reduces the basket skew relative to individual stock skews**.

When stocks are fully correlated ($\rho_{ij} = 1$), the basket behaves like a single stock and the skew equals the **weighted average** of individual skews. When correlation falls, diversification kicks in and the basket distribution becomes more Gaussian by CLT arguments.

The **skew ratio** — basket skew divided by weighted average single-stock skew — can be approximated as:

$$
\text{SR}(\bar\rho) \approx \bar\rho \cdot \frac{\sigma_B}{\sum_i w_i \sigma_i} \leq 1
$$

since by Cauchy-Schwarz: $\sigma_B \leq \sum_i w_i \sigma_i$.

</Section>

<Section number="05" title="Practical Implementation">

In practice, reconstruction is done via **Monte Carlo simulation** of the basket under calibrated single-stock surfaces:

1. Calibrate each $S_i$ to a stochastic vol model (SABR, Heston, or SVI parametrization)
2. Specify a correlation matrix $\rho$ (from historical estimation or index option calibration)
3. Simulate $B_T = \sum_i w_i S_i(T)$ under the joint distribution
4. Extract the smile of $B_T$ from simulated payoffs via regression or histogram

<Callout type="note">
For fast calibration loops, the moment-based approach (Sections 2–3) is preferred as a warm-start. The Monte Carlo step validates and refines the result.
</Callout>

### Correlation Sensitivity

The key risk parameter for dispersion traders is $\partial \sigma_B / \partial \rho_{ij}$:

$$
\frac{\partial \sigma_B}{\partial \rho_{ij}} = \frac{w_i w_j \sigma_i \sigma_j}{\sigma_B}
$$

Large-weight stocks with high individual vols dominate the sensitivity. This is the core of **correlation risk** in dispersion books.

| Parameter | Impact on Basket Skew | Sign |
|-----------|----------------------|------|
| ↑ Correlation | Increases basket skew | + |
| ↑ Weight concentration | Increases basket skew | + |
| ↑ Stock count (equal weights) | Decreases basket skew (CLT) | − |
| ↑ Individual stock skew | Increases basket skew | + |

<Callout type="result" title="DISPERSION TRADE INTUITION">
Long dispersion (long single-stock vol, short index vol) profits when realized correlation is lower than the implied level embedded in the index smile. The skew differential is the forward-looking indicator of this trade's value.
</Callout>

</Section>
