<Section number="01" title="The Autocall Structure">

An **autocall** (or auto-callable) is a structured note that automatically redeems early if the underlying crosses a **barrier level** on one of several **observation dates**.

## Payoff Description

Let $\{T_1, T_2, \ldots, T_n\}$ be the observation dates and $B_k$ the corresponding barriers (typically set at 100% of initial spot, or higher). The product:

- **Auto-redeems** at $T_k$ if $S_{T_k} \geq B_k$, paying coupon $C_k$ plus notional
- **Otherwise continues** to the next observation
- **At maturity** $T_n$, pays a capital-protected (or capital-at-risk) amount depending on final spot

<MathBlock label="Autocall Payoff" number="1.1">

$$
V = \sum_{k=1}^n \mathbf{1}\left[S_{T_k} \geq B_k,\ S_{T_j} < B_j\ \forall j < k\right]\cdot(N + C_k) \cdot D(0, T_k) + \text{barrier payoff}
$$

where $N$ is notional, $C_k$ is coupon, $D(0,T)$ is the discount factor.

</MathBlock>

<Callout type="insight">
  The key risk of autocalls lies in their **path-dependency**: the product only survives if the autocall barriers are not breached at all earlier dates. This creates a complex interaction between vol surface shape and barrier proximity.
</Callout>

The **barrier feature** makes autocalls extremely sensitive to:
1. The **forward vol** (vol between observation dates)
2. The **vol of vol** (how the smile evolves)
3. The **skew** near barrier levels

</Section>

<Section number="02" title="Local Volatility — LV Model">

## Dupire's Local Vol

In the **Local Volatility** model (Dupire 1994), the spot dynamics are:

<MathBlock label="LV Dynamics" number="2.1">

$$
\frac{dS_t}{S_t} = \mu(t)\,dt + \sigma_{\text{loc}}(S_t, t)\,dW_t
$$

</MathBlock>

The local vol function $\sigma_{\text{loc}}(K, T)$ is uniquely determined by Dupire's formula from the observed call prices $C(K,T)$:

$$
\sigma_{\text{loc}}^2(K,T) = \frac{\partial_T C + (r-q)K\,\partial_K C + q\,C}{\tfrac{1}{2}K^2\,\partial_{KK}C}
$$

### LV and Forward Volatility

A fundamental property: in the LV model, **the forward variance** $\mathbb{E}[\sigma_{\text{loc}}^2(S_u, u) | \mathcal{F}_t]$ is determined by today's surface. This means the model **has no free parameters for forward vols** — they are fully determined by the initial smile.

<Callout type="warning">
  In LV, the forward skew decays much faster than implied by market intuition. For a 3-month forward starting option with 1-year total maturity, LV predicts a forward skew that is roughly **half** the spot skew. This is unrealistically low for most equity underlyings.
</Callout>

### Smile Dynamics in LV

The **"sticky-strike" vs "sticky-delta" debate** is critical here. In LV:

- The smile moves with the spot — as $S$ rises, the effective vol at a fixed strike falls (sticky-strike behavior is absent)
- This causes the **delta** to differ from a Black-Scholes hedge, and the LV delta is generally **too low** (put overhedge)

</Section>

<Section number="03" title="Local Stochastic Volatility — LSV Model">

## The LSV Framework

**Local Stochastic Volatility** models combine:
- A stochastic vol driver (Heston, SABR-like)
- A local vol leverage function $L(S,t)$ that ensures perfect vanilla calibration

<MathBlock label="LSV Dynamics" number="3.1">

$$
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{dS_t}{S_t} = \mu_t\,dt + L(S_t,t)\,\sqrt{V_t}\,dW_t^S \\[8pt]
dV_t = \kappa(\theta - V_t)\,dt + \xi\,\sqrt{V_t}\,dW_t^V \\[6pt]
d\langle W^S, W^V\rangle_t = \rho\,dt
\end{cases}
$$

</MathBlock>

The leverage function $L(S,t)$ is determined by the **particle method** (Guyon-Henry-Labordère) or by the **McKean-Vlasov SDE** inversion condition:

$$
L^2(S,t) = \frac{\sigma_{\text{loc}}^2(S,t)}{\mathbb{E}[V_t \,|\, S_t = S]}
$$

This ensures that marginals of $S_t$ match the market-implied distribution exactly.

<Callout type="insight">
  The leverage function $L$ acts as a **residual** between the target vanilla surface and what the pure stochastic vol process would produce. When $L \equiv 1$, the model reduces to pure Heston; when $\xi \to 0$, it reduces to pure LV.
</Callout>

</Section>

In LSV, the **vol of vol** ($\xi$) and **mean reversion** ($\kappa$) control how much the smile "flattens" over time. Because LSV has genuine stochastic vol dynamics, it retains more curvature in the forward smile.

### 2. Barrier Transition Probabilities

For an autocall with barriers $B_k$ near current spot, the key pricing metric is:

<MathBlock label="Transition Probability" number="4.1">

$$
p_k = \mathbb{P}\left[S_{T_k} \geq B_k \,\Big|\, S_{T_j} < B_j,\ j < k\right]
$$

</MathBlock>

The **model spread** in autocall NPV typically follows:

$$
\Delta V_{\text{LV-LSV}} \approx N \cdot \text{coupon} \cdot \sum_k D(0,T_k) \cdot \Delta p_k
$$

Where $\Delta p_k = p_k^{LV} - p_k^{LSV}$ depends heavily on the moneyness of the barriers and the time between observation dates.

### 3. Vega Hedging Differences

The **vega profile** is qualitatively different across models:

| Sensitivity | Local Vol | LSV |
|------------|-----------|-----|
| Vega (spot vol) | Positive, decays fast | Positive, more persistent |
| Volga (vega convexity) | Near zero | Significant |
| Vanna (vol-spot correlation risk) | Model-implied only | Explicit via $\rho$ |
| Forward vol risk | None (fully captured) | Explicit $\xi$ sensitivity |

<Callout type="result">
  **Rule of thumb**: For autocalls with barriers set at 100% (at-the-money observation), the LSV price tends to be **5–15 bps lower** than LV for a typical 1-year note, primarily due to the higher forward implied vol above spot in LSV. For down-barrier products (barrier < 100%), the sign can reverse.
</Callout>

<Section number="05" title="Calibration Strategy">

## Practical Calibration

### Two-Step Approach

**Step 1 — Vanilla calibration (exact)**

Calibrate the leverage function $L(S,t)$ so that the LSV model reprices all liquid vanillas exactly. This is done via the McKean-Vlasov condition and requires solving a forward Kolmogorov PDE or using a particle simulation:

$$
\text{Minimize} \sum_{K_i,T_j} \left|C^{\text{model}}(K_i,T_j) - C^{\text{market}}(K_i,T_j)\right|^2
$$

subject to $L$ being a smooth, positive function.

**Step 2 — Exotic calibration (model selection)**

Choose $(\xi, \kappa, \rho)$ of the stochastic vol component to match:
- Prices of liquid exotics (forward vols, barrier options if available)
- Historical behavior of the smile dynamics

<Callout type="note">
  In practice, $\rho$ (vol-spot correlation) is calibrated to the vanilla skew as a first pass. $\xi$ (vol of vol) is calibrated to the curvature (butterfly). $\kappa$ controls the term structure of the forward smile.
</Callout>

### Stability Considerations

The leverage function computation is numerically challenging. Key issues:

1. **Density estimation**: $\mathbb{E}[V_t | S_t = S]$ requires kernel smoothing of the simulated particle cloud
2. **Interpolation**: $L$ must be smooth and non-negative; negative values indicate calibration instability
3. **Regularization**: Add Tikhonov regularization to prevent overfitting to noisy market data

```python
# Pseudocode: Particle method for L(S,t)
for each time step t_k:
    # Propagate N particles (S_i, V_i) under pure SV dynamics
    propagate_particles(S, V, params)

    # Estimate conditional expectation via kernel smoothing
    E_V_given_S = kernel_regression(S_particles, V_particles, bandwidth=h)

    # Compute leverage
    L_sq = sigma_loc(S, t) ** 2 / E_V_given_S
    L = np.sqrt(np.maximum(L_sq, 0))  # ensure non-negative

    # Apply to drift particles
    apply_leverage(S, V, L)
```

</Section>

<Section number="06" title="Model Risk and Reserves">

## Model Risk in Exotic Books

Given the LV/LSV discrepancy, risk managers typically assign a **model reserve** equal to a fraction of the pricing difference:

$$
\text{Reserve} = \alpha \cdot |\Delta V_{\text{LV-LSV}}|, \quad \alpha \in [0.3, 0.7]
$$

The reserve is held against **future model recalibration risk** and **hedging error** arising from model mismatch.

<Callout type="warning">
  Using LV alone for autocall hedging can lead to systematic **short vega positions** that are not apparent from the standard delta-vega report. The "missing vega" in the forward smile creates a P&L bleed that shows up as unexplained theta losses over time.
</Callout>

### Monitoring and Stress Tests

For robust exotic book management:

1. **Daily P&L explain**: decompose into delta, vega, vanna, volga contributions
2. **Model comparison**: run LV and LSV prices daily, track the spread
3. **Scenario analysis**: stress vol surface by ±10% parallel shift, ±5% skew twist
4. **Forward vol monitoring**: check that forward implied vols are within historical bounds

The **forward vol ratio** $\text{FVR}(T_1, T_2) = \sigma_{\text{fwd}}(T_1,T_2) / \sigma_{\text{spot}}(T_2)$ is a key early-warning indicator for model stress.

</Section>
