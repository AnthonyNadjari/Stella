Basket Skew Reconstruction

How to reconstruct the implied skew of a basket from single-stock volatilities, correlations, and higher-order effects.

01

Problem Statement

A basket option written on nnn underlyings with weights wiw_iwi​ has a payoff driven by the aggregate move of the basket. When pricing exotic derivatives on baskets — dispersion trades, worst-of structures, rainbow options — a key input is the implied volatility surface of the basket itself.

In practice, liquid vanilla options exist on the individual underlyings, not on the basket directly. The task is to reconstruct the basket's implied vol surface — and in particular its skew — from single-stock information and a correlation structure.

◈INSIGHT

The basket skew is not a simple weighted average of single-stock skews. It depends non-linearly on correlations, and the reconstruction error can be significant for short-dated options or highly skewed underlyings.

The basket price at time TTT is:

BT=∑i=1nwiSi(T)B_T = \sum_{i=1}^n w_i S_i(T)BT​=i=1∑n​wi​Si​(T)

with ∑iwi=1\sum_i w_i = 1∑i​wi​=1. We define the basket log-return as XB=log⁡(BT/B0)X_B = \log(B_T / B_0)XB​=log(BT​/B0​).

02

First-Order Approximation

Under a Gaussian correlation model (all underlyings lognormal), the basket variance is:

σB2≈∑i,jwiwjρijσiσj\sigma_B^2 \approx \sum_{i,j} w_i w_j \rho_{ij} \sigma_i \sigma_jσB2​≈i,j∑​wi​wj​ρij​σi​σj​
Basket ATM Variance(2.1)
σB2=w⊤Σw\sigma_B^2 = \mathbf{w}^\top \Sigma \mathbf{w}σB2​=w⊤Σw

where Σij=ρij σi σj\Sigma_{ij} = \rho_{ij}\,\sigma_i\,\sigma_jΣij​=ρij​σi​σj​ is the covariance matrix.

The first-order skew of the basket derives from a cumulant expansion. Define the skewness of stock iii as κ3(i)\kappa_3^{(i)}κ3(i)​. Then:

κ3B≈∑i,j,kwiwjwk CijkσB3\kappa_3^B \approx \frac{\sum_{i,j,k} w_i w_j w_k \,C_{ijk}}{\sigma_B^3}κ3B​≈σB3​∑i,j,k​wi​wj​wk​Cijk​​

where Cijk=E[δXi δXj δXk]C_{ijk} = \mathbb{E}[\delta X_i \, \delta X_j \, \delta X_k]Cijk​=E[δXi​δXj​δXk​] is the third-order co-cumulant tensor.

⚠WARNING

The co-cumulant tensor CijkC_{ijk}Cijk​ is rarely observable directly. In practice, one assumes a diagonal approximation where cross-stock third moments are set to zero. This underestimates basket skew when correlations are high.

03

Skew via Moment Matching

A cleaner method is to match moments of a parametric distribution to those computed from the basket's cumulants.

Step 1 — Compute basket moments up to order 4 via Isserlis' theorem.

Step 2 — Fit a Gram-Charlier expansion of the basket density:

Gram-Charlier Density(3.1)
fB(x)=ϕ(x)[1+κ3B6H3(x)+κ4B24H4(x)+⋯ ]f_B(x) = \phi(x)\left[1 + \frac{\kappa_3^B}{6}H_3(x) + \frac{\kappa_4^B}{24}H_4(x) + \cdots \right]fB​(x)=ϕ(x)[1+6κ3B​​H3​(x)+24κ4B​​H4​(x)+⋯]

where HkH_kHk​ are Hermite polynomials and ϕ\phiϕ is the standard normal density.

Step 3 — Extract the implied vol smile. The basket skew approximation:

σimp(k)≈σB[1+κ3B6k+κ4B−324k2]\sigma_{\text{imp}}(k) \approx \sigma_B\left[1 + \frac{\kappa_3^B}{6}k + \frac{\kappa_4^B - 3}{24}k^2\right]σimp​(k)≈σB​[1+6κ3B​​k+24κ4B​−3​k2]

where k=log⁡(K/F)/σBTk = \log(K/F)/\sigma_B\sqrt{T}k=log(K/F)/σB​T​ is the normalized moneyness.

✦KEY RESULT

The basket skew is proportional to κ3B\kappa_3^Bκ3B​. For a basket of negatively skewed stocks under positive correlation, the basket skew is weaker than the average single-stock skew — a fundamental result for dispersion trading.

04

Correlation Weighting of Skew

One of the most important practical results: higher correlation reduces the basket skew relative to individual stock skews.

When stocks are fully correlated (ρij=1\rho_{ij} = 1ρij​=1), the basket behaves like a single stock and the skew equals the weighted average of individual skews. When correlation falls, diversification kicks in and the basket distribution becomes more Gaussian by CLT arguments.

The skew ratio — basket skew divided by weighted average single-stock skew — can be approximated as:

SR(ρˉ)≈ρˉ⋅σB∑iwiσi≤1\text{SR}(\bar\rho) \approx \bar\rho \cdot \frac{\sigma_B}{\sum_i w_i \sigma_i} \leq 1SR(ρˉ​)≈ρˉ​⋅∑i​wi​σi​σB​​≤1

since by Cauchy-Schwarz: σB≤∑iwiσi\sigma_B \leq \sum_i w_i \sigma_iσB​≤∑i​wi​σi​.

05

Practical Implementation

In practice, reconstruction is done via Monte Carlo simulation of the basket under calibrated single-stock surfaces:

  1. Calibrate each SiS_iSi​ to a stochastic vol model (SABR, Heston, or SVI parametrization)
  2. Specify a correlation matrix ρ\rhoρ (from historical estimation or index option calibration)
  3. Simulate BT=∑iwiSi(T)B_T = \sum_i w_i S_i(T)BT​=∑i​wi​Si​(T) under the joint distribution
  4. Extract the smile of BTB_TBT​ from simulated payoffs via regression or histogram
◎NOTE

For fast calibration loops, the moment-based approach (Sections 2–3) is preferred as a warm-start. The Monte Carlo step validates and refines the result.

Correlation Sensitivity

The key risk parameter for dispersion traders is ∂σB/∂ρij\partial \sigma_B / \partial \rho_{ij}∂σB​/∂ρij​:

∂σB∂ρij=wiwjσiσjσB\frac{\partial \sigma_B}{\partial \rho_{ij}} = \frac{w_i w_j \sigma_i \sigma_j}{\sigma_B}∂ρij​∂σB​​=σB​wi​wj​σi​σj​​

Large-weight stocks with high individual vols dominate the sensitivity. This is the core of correlation risk in dispersion books.

✦DISPERSION TRADE INTUITION

Long dispersion (long single-stock vol, short index vol) profits when realized correlation is lower than the implied level embedded in the index smile. The skew differential is the forward-looking indicator of this trade's value.
