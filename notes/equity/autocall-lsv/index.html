Autocall Pricing — LSV vs LV

A comparative analysis of Local Stochastic Volatility (LSV) and Local Volatility (LV) models for autocallable structured products, including barrier sensitivities and vega hedging differences.

#autocall#LSV#local-vol#stochastic-vol#structured-products#barrier
01

The Autocall Structure

An autocall is a structured note that automatically redeems early if the underlying crosses a barrier level on one of several observation dates.

The product:

  • Auto-redeems at TkT_kTk​ if STk≥BkS_{T_k} \geq B_kSTk​​≥Bk​, paying coupon CkC_kCk​ plus notional
  • Otherwise continues to the next observation
  • At maturity TnT_nTn​, pays a capital-protected (or capital-at-risk) amount
Autocall Payoff(1.1)
V=∑k=1n1[STk≥Bk, STj<Bj ∀j<k]⋅(N+Ck)⋅D(0,Tk)V = \sum_{k=1}^n \mathbf{1}\left[S_{T_k} \geq B_k,\ S_{T_j} < B_j\ \forall j < k\right]\cdot(N + C_k) \cdot D(0, T_k)V=k=1∑n​1[STk​​≥Bk​, STj​​<Bj​ ∀j<k]⋅(N+Ck​)⋅D(0,Tk​)
◈INSIGHT

The key risk of autocalls lies in their path-dependency: the product only survives if the autocall barriers are not breached at all earlier dates. This creates a complex interaction between vol surface shape and barrier proximity.

The barrier feature makes autocalls extremely sensitive to:

  1. The forward vol (vol between observation dates)
  2. The vol of vol (how the smile evolves)
  3. The skew near barrier levels
02

Local Volatility — LV Model

In the Local Volatility model (Dupire 1994), the spot dynamics are:

LV Dynamics(2.1)
dStSt=μ(t) dt+σloc(St,t) dWt\frac{dS_t}{S_t} = \mu(t)\,dt + \sigma_{\text{loc}}(S_t, t)\,dW_tSt​dSt​​=μ(t)dt+σloc​(St​,t)dWt​

The local vol function σloc(K,T)\sigma_{\text{loc}}(K, T)σloc​(K,T) is uniquely determined by Dupire's formula:

σloc2(K,T)=∂TC+(r−q)K ∂KC+q C12K2 ∂KKC\sigma_{\text{loc}}^2(K,T) = \frac{\partial_T C + (r-q)K\,\partial_K C + q\,C}{\tfrac{1}{2}K^2\,\partial_{KK}C}σloc2​(K,T)=21​K2∂KK​C∂T​C+(r−q)K∂K​C+qC​

In LV, the forward variance E[σloc2(Su,u)∣Ft]\mathbb{E}[\sigma_{\text{loc}}^2(S_u, u) | \mathcal{F}_t]E[σloc2​(Su​,u)∣Ft​] is fully determined by today's surface — the model has no free parameters for forward vols.

⚠WARNING

In LV, the forward skew decays much faster than implied by market intuition. For a 3-month forward starting option with 1-year total maturity, LV predicts a forward skew roughly half the spot skew. This is unrealistically low for most equity underlyings.

03

Local Stochastic Volatility — LSV Model

Local Stochastic Volatility models combine a stochastic vol driver with a local vol leverage function L(S,t)L(S,t)L(S,t) that ensures perfect vanilla calibration.

LSV Dynamics(3.1)
{dStSt=μt dt+L(St,t) Vt dWtSdVt=κ(θ−Vt) dt+ξ Vt dWtVd⟨WS,WV⟩t=ρ dt\begin{cases} \dfrac{dS_t}{S_t} = \mu_t\,dt + L(S_t,t)\,\sqrt{V_t}\,dW_t^S \\[8pt] dV_t = \kappa(\theta - V_t)\,dt + \xi\,\sqrt{V_t}\,dW_t^V \\[6pt] d\langle W^S, W^V\rangle_t = \rho\,dt \end{cases}⎩⎨⎧​St​dSt​​=μt​dt+L(St​,t)Vt​​dWtS​dVt​=κ(θ−Vt​)dt+ξVt​​dWtV​d⟨WS,WV⟩t​=ρdt​

The leverage function L(S,t)L(S,t)L(S,t) is determined by the McKean-Vlasov inversion condition:

L2(S,t)=σloc2(S,t)E[Vt ∣ St=S]L^2(S,t) = \frac{\sigma_{\text{loc}}^2(S,t)}{\mathbb{E}[V_t \,|\, S_t = S]}L2(S,t)=E[Vt​∣St​=S]σloc2​(S,t)​

This ensures marginals of StS_tSt​ match the market-implied distribution exactly.

◈INSIGHT

The leverage function LLL acts as a residual between the target vanilla surface and what the pure stochastic vol process would produce. When L≡1L \equiv 1L≡1, the model reduces to pure Heston; when ξ→0\xi \to 0ξ→0, it reduces to pure LV.

Key Differences for Autocall Pricing

1. Forward Skew

Autocalls are sensitive to the conditional distribution of STk∣STk−1<Bk−1S_{T_k} | S_{T_{k-1}} < B_{k-1}STk​​∣STk−1​​<Bk−1​, which is determined by the forward smile.

2. Barrier Transition Probabilities

Transition Probability(4.1)
pk=P[STk≥Bk ∣ STj<Bj, j<k]p_k = \mathbb{P}\left[S_{T_k} \geq B_k \,\Big|\, S_{T_j} < B_j,\ j < k\right]pk​=P[STk​​≥Bk​​STj​​<Bj​, j<k]

The model spread in autocall NPV:

ΔVLV-LSV≈N⋅coupon⋅∑kD(0,Tk)⋅Δpk\Delta V_{\text{LV-LSV}} \approx N \cdot \text{coupon} \cdot \sum_k D(0,T_k) \cdot \Delta p_kΔVLV-LSV​≈N⋅coupon⋅k∑​D(0,Tk​)⋅Δpk​

✦PRICING RULE OF THUMB

For autocalls with barriers at 100% (ATM observations), the LSV price tends to be 5–15 bps lower than LV for a typical 1-year note, primarily due to the higher forward implied vol above spot in LSV. For down-barrier products (barrier < 100%), the sign can reverse.

05

Calibration Strategy

Two-Step Approach

Step 1 — Vanilla calibration (exact)

Calibrate L(S,t)L(S,t)L(S,t) so the LSV model reprices all liquid vanillas exactly via the McKean-Vlasov condition:

min⁡L∑Ki,Tj∣Cmodel(Ki,Tj)−Cmarket(Ki,Tj)∣2\min_{L} \sum_{K_i,T_j} \left|C^{\text{model}}(K_i,T_j) - C^{\text{market}}(K_i,T_j)\right|^2Lmin​Ki​,Tj​∑​​Cmodel(Ki​,Tj​)−Cmarket(Ki​,Tj​)​2

Step 2 — Exotic calibration (model selection)

Choose (ξ,κ,ρ)(\xi, \kappa, \rho)(ξ,κ,ρ) of the stochastic vol component to match liquid exotics and historical smile dynamics.

◎NOTE

In practice: ρ\rhoρ is calibrated to vanilla skew, ξ\xiξ to curvature (butterfly), κ\kappaκ controls the term structure of the forward smile.

Numerical Implementation

# Particle method for L(S,t)
for t_k in time_steps:
    propagate_particles(S, V, params)
    E_V_given_S = kernel_regression(S_particles, V_particles)
    L_sq = sigma_loc(S, t_k) ** 2 / E_V_given_S
    L = np.sqrt(np.maximum(L_sq, 0))
    apply_leverage(S, V, L)
06

Model Risk and Reserves

Given the LV/LSV discrepancy, risk managers assign a model reserve:

Reserve=α⋅∣ΔVLV-LSV∣,α∈[0.3,0.7]\text{Reserve} = \alpha \cdot |\Delta V_{\text{LV-LSV}}|, \quad \alpha \in [0.3, 0.7]Reserve=α⋅∣ΔVLV-LSV​∣,α∈[0.3,0.7]
⚠WARNING

Using LV alone for autocall hedging can lead to systematic short vega positions not apparent from the standard delta-vega report. The missing vega in the forward smile creates a P&L bleed that shows up as unexplained theta losses over time.
